The Great Tech War

By Vera Holloway

What age should my child get a phone? One of the most common questions I am asked as a counselor is, what age should my kid get a phone? Here is my inside perspective from years of clinical mental health counseling and currently living in the season of parenting pre-teens. This is a war like no other — and most parents are walking into it unarmed.

The Reality We're Living In

According to Pew Research (2018), 95% of teenagers have access to a smartphone, and 45% report being online almost constantly. This is not a fringe issue. This is the water our children are swimming in — and most of them have no idea how to navigate it.

I want to be clear: this is not an anti-technology article. Technology is not the enemy. The issue is that we are handing our kids extraordinarily powerful devices without equipping them spiritually or emotionally for what they will encounter. That gap is where the damage happens.

The Two Missing Links

In my years of counseling, I have identified two areas where parents — even good, loving, attentive parents — consistently fall short. I call them the two missing links. When children are deprived in these areas, they become far more vulnerable to unhealthy attachments — to screens, to relationships, to substances. The void gets filled somehow. Our job as parents is to fill it with the right things first.

1

Spiritual Neglect

We cannot simply hope our children absorb faith by proximity. We must actively, intentionally guide them spiritually. That means more than Sunday morning attendance. It means training them for action — teaching them how to pray, how to hear God's voice, how to measure what they encounter in the world against the truth of Scripture. A child who knows who they are in Christ is infinitely harder to manipulate than one who doesn't. Spiritual formation is our greatest parenting tool, and we are leaving it on the table.

2

Sexual Neglect

Silence is not safety. When parents avoid conversations about sex, children don't stay ignorant — they simply get their information somewhere else. And that somewhere else is increasingly the internet. Research consistently shows that those who are best informed make the best decisions. This is not about robbing childhood innocence. It is about being the first and most trusted voice in your child's life on this topic. Start age-appropriate conversations early, build on them over time, and create a home where these conversations are normal — not shameful, not awkward, just real.

Deprivation in either of these areas creates a vacuum. Children who lack spiritual grounding and healthy sexual education are far more susceptible to unhealthy attachments — including the compulsive, isolating kind that technology so easily enables.

A 6-Point Plan for Parents

So what do we actually do? Here is the practical framework I give to parents in my practice:

  1. Don't neglect your child's spiritual needs. Make faith formation an active, daily practice — not a passive Sunday habit.
  2. Educate about sex in stages, starting young. Use age-appropriate language from the very beginning. Your child is never too young to learn the correct names for body parts, or that their body belongs to them.
  3. Don't cave to culture. "But everyone else has one" is not a reason. You are the parent. You set the standard in your home.
  4. Have a plan before the pre-teen years. Don't wait until your child is begging for a phone to figure out what your family values are. Know your convictions before the pressure comes.
  5. Provide replacement activities. Children turn to screens when they are bored, lonely, or anxious. Make sure they have real relationships, creative outlets, physical activity, and meaningful responsibilities.
  6. Vera's personal recommendation on timing: Consider an iPad at ages 10–12 with strict parental controls, and a phone at ages 12–14 — with ongoing monitoring, open conversations, and clear household rules about usage.

You Can Win This War

This is not a battle you have to lose. Parents who are proactive, who communicate openly, and who fill their children's lives with purpose and connection will raise kids who are far better equipped to handle the digital world — and everything in it.

You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be present, be intentional, and be willing to have the hard conversations before someone else does.

You've got this. And your kids are worth the fight.
